Module 7: Mass Storage Management
Overview:
This module examines Mass-Storage Structure and Disk Scheduling in modern operating systems, focusing on how data is physically arranged, accessed, and optimized on secondary storage systems. It begins with an overview of Magnetic Disks, discussing physical components like platters, tracks, sectors, and the read-write heads that handle data access. The module then introduces key performance metrics, explaining how seek time, rotational latency, and transfer rates combine to form the total Disk Access Time. A major focus is placed on Disk Scheduling Algorithms, which are critical for minimizing mechanical head movement and improving system throughput. It provides detailed, step-by-step explanations and mathematical examples for First-Come First-Served (FCFS), Shortest Seek Time First (SSTF), SCAN (the Elevator Algorithm), and C-SCAN (Circular SCAN). Finally, it addresses how to calculate Total Head Movement to evaluate algorithm efficiency, highlighting how operating systems minimize latency to optimize mass-storage performance.
What is Mass Storage?
Mass storage, often called secondary storage, refers to the computer devices and media engineered to hold vast quantities of data permanently and non-volatily. While main memory (RAM) is fast but temporary, clearing completely whenever the system loses power, mass storage acts as the computer's long-term digital warehouse. It retains everything from the operating system and core software applications to personal user files, ensuring data survives across system reboots. Because it is physically positioned further from the CPU and frequently relies on complex flash memory controllers or mechanical components, mass storage operates at significantly slower data transfer speeds than RAM, but compensates by offering massive capacities at a fraction of the cost.

Disk Structure A magnetic disk system relies on a stack of flat, circular platters fixed to a central rotating shaft (or spindle). Both surfaces of each platter are coated with a magnetizable material where data is permanently recorded. Logically, the surface of each platter is mapped into concentric circles called tracks, which are further subdivided into smaller arcs known as sectors. The sector serves as the fundamental, smallest addressable unit of data storage on the drive. When multiple platters are stacked, the collection of tracks at the exact same radius across all surfaces forms a vertical cylinder. Operating systems often group several sectors together into a single logical unit called a cluster to manage file allocation more efficiently.
Components and Parts
• Rotating Shaft (Spindle):
Driven by a high-speed motor, this central axis spins the stacked platters at a constant speed (typically measured in revolutions per second or RPM). This continuous rotation ensures that the recorded data sectors pass beneath the drive's sensors for constant accessibility.

• Read-Write Head:
A tiny electromagnetic component positioned just micro-inches above the platter surface. Its primary function is to detect (read) or alter (write) the magnetic orientation of the sectors directly beneath it, converting physical magnetic patterns into binary data and vice versa.

• Arm Assembly (Actuator):
A mechanical bracket framework that holds the read-write heads in place. It moves radially back and forth across the surfaces of the spinning platters, shifting the heads precisely between different tracks to locate specific data.

Performance: • Seek Time: The duration required for the mechanical arm assembly to physically move the read-write head from its current position to the specific track containing the requested data. This represents the primary mechanical delay because it involves physical movement across the disk radius.

• Latency Time (Rotational Latency): The delay that occurs after the head reaches the correct track, during which the drive waits for the spinning platter to bring the target sector directly underneath the read-write head. This time is directly dependent on the rotational speed of the spindle motor.

• Transfer Time: The electronic phase of the operation, representing the actual speed at which data bits are read from or written to the magnetic surface and transmitted between the disk drive and the system's main memory.
Two Ways of Reading and Writing on the Disks
Constant Linear Velocity (CLV) • In disks using CLV, the density of bits per unit length is uniform across every track on the media. Because the outer tracks have a larger circumference, they contain more data bits and more sectors than the shorter inner tracks. To keep the rate of data moving past the read-write head completely constant, the drive motor must dynamically change its speed. The drive increases its rotation speed as the head moves from the outer tracks toward the inner tracks to compensate for the decreasing track length. This variable-speed method is exclusively used in optical storage media such as CD-ROM and DVD-ROM drives.

Constant Angular Velocity (CAV) • In disks using CAV, the number of bits per track is uniform across the entire disk, meaning every track contains a constant number of sectors. Because the larger outer tracks hold the exact same amount of data as the shorter inner tracks, the physical density of the bits automatically increases from the outer tracks to the inner tracks. Because the sectors are aligned at fixed geometric angles, the disk rotation speed stays completely constant regardless of where the read-write head is positioned. This fixed-speed method is the standard technique used in magnetic storage devices like hard disks and floppy disks.

Disk Scheduling Disk scheduling is the process of managing and prioritizing input/output (I/O) requests that arrive for the hard disk. Since mechanical hard drives rely on a physical arm assembly to move to a specific track (seek time) and a spindle to rotate a sector under the head (rotational latency), random requests can cause the drive head to move back and forth inefficiently, severely slowing down performance.

First Come, First Serve (FCFS) Scheduling • This is the simplest form of disk scheduling. It services I/O requests strictly in the order they arrive in the queue, without regard for the current position of the disk head.

• Pros & Cons: It is completely fair and prevents starvation (every request is guaranteed to be serviced). However, it results in massive, wild head movements across the disk, creating high total head travel and slow average response times.
Example:
Shortest Seek Time First Scheduling • This algorithm selects the request that is closest to the current position of the read-write head, minimizing the immediate seek time.

• Pros & Cons: It significantly reduces the total head movement compared to FCFS, boosting overall throughput. The major disadvantage is starvation; if a steady stream of requests keeps arriving near the current head position, requests on the far edges of the disk may wait indefinitely.
Example:
SCAN Scheduling • The disk head starts at one end of the disk and moves toward the other end, servicing requests as it reaches each track. When it hits the very edge of the disk, it reverses direction and moves back toward the opposite end, continuing to service remaining requests.

• Pros & Cons: It eliminates starvation and provides a much lower variance in response time compared to SSTF. However, it can be inefficient because the head travels all the way to the end of the disk even if there are no pending requests waiting at the extreme boundary.
Example:
C-SCAN Scheduling • Designed to provide a more uniform waiting time, C-SCAN moves the head from one end of the disk to the other, servicing requests along the way. However, when it reaches the end boundary, it immediately wraps around to the beginning boundary without servicing any requests on the return trip, and then restarts its scanning motion.

• Pros & Cons: It provides a highly predictable and uniform delay for all requests across the entire disk surface. The downside is that the complete reset across the disk adds an unserviced travel delay.
Example:
LOOK Scheduling • The read-write head moves in one direction servicing requests until there are no more pending requests ahead of its current position. Instead of traveling all the way to the absolute physical edge of the disk, it immediately stops and reverses direction on the spot to service requests waiting behind it.

• It prevents the starvation of requests on the extreme inner and outer tracks while remaining more efficient than SCAN by eliminating wasted mechanical travel to empty disk boundaries; however, it still results in unequal waiting times because tracks near the middle of the disk are naturally visited more frequently than those on the extreme edges.
C-LOOK Scheduling • The head services requests in only one direction (e.g., from lowest track number to highest). Once it services the highest requested track, it performs a single rapid return trip straight back to the lowest requested track in the queue—without servicing any tracks on the way back and without traveling to the physical edges of the disk.

• Pros & Cons: It provides a highly uniform and predictable waiting time for all requests across the disk surface while maximizing efficiency by restricting all mechanical sweeps strictly to the span of active requests; however, the completely unserviced return trip across the disk creates an extra idle delay before it can resume processing requests at the starting end.
Total Head Movement By subtracting the smaller track number from the larger track number at each step, you calculate the absolute distance traveled for that leg of the journey. Summing all those individual movements together gives you the Total Head Movement, which directly reflects the mechanical seek time delay.
